#Task Estimation in Scrum

##Bad Practices
Poor estimation practices can reduce delivery reliability and create confusion within a team.
In a small startup environment, this can lead to missed deadlines, unrealistic expectations, and pressure that affects quality.

Common bad practices in task estimation include:
- Estimating work without discussing it as a team
- Letting one person decide estimates alone
- Treating estimates as exact promises instead of informed predictions
- Ignoring complexity, risk, and uncertainty
- Estimating tasks that are too large or unclear
- Changing estimates without explaining why
- Rushing estimation just to move work forward
- Using estimation to judge individual performance instead of planning work

These practices make estimation less useful and can lead to inconsistent delivery and poor collaboration.

##Team Rules
To keep estimation consistent and practical, the team should follow these rules:

- Estimate tasks together as a team
- Make sure each task is clear before estimating it
- Break large tasks into smaller pieces where possible
- Consider complexity, effort, and uncertainty when estimating
- Use the same estimation approach across the team
- Ask questions before agreeing on an estimate
- Review estimates when new information appears
- Treat estimates as planning tools, not fixed guarantees

These rules help the team create shared understanding and improve predictability.

##Checklist
Before agreeing on a task estimate, the team should ask:
- Is the task clearly defined?
- Does everyone understand what needs to be done?
- Has the task been broken down enough to estimate properly?
- Are there any dependencies or blockers?
- Are there technical risks or unknowns?
- Has the team discussed the effort involved?
- Is the estimate based on shared understanding?
- Does the estimate reflect complexity and uncertainty, not just ideal time?
